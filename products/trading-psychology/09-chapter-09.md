# Chapter 9: Recovery Protocol — Coming Back After Losses

## The Anatomy of a Drawdown

Every trader experiences drawdowns. It is not a question of if, but when and how deep. The difference between traders who recover and traders who blow up is not the drawdown itself — it is how they respond to it.

A drawdown follows a predictable emotional arc:

**Stage 1: Denial (Day 1-3)**
"This is normal volatility. My system is fine. It will come back."
If the drawdown is within your expected range, this is actually correct. The danger is staying in denial when the drawdown exceeds your expected parameters.

**Stage 2: Frustration (Day 3-7)**
"Why is everything going against me? Am I doing something wrong?" You start questioning your strategy, second-guessing entries, and feeling the pull of revenge trading. This is the highest-risk stage for impulsive decisions.

**Stage 3: Fear (Week 2-3)**
"What if I have lost my edge? What if the market has changed?" Position sizes shrink not because of rules, but because of fear. You start skipping valid setups because you cannot tolerate another loss.

**Stage 4: Acceptance (Week 3-4)**
"This is part of trading. My system has drawdowns, and this is one of them. I need to follow the process." This is where recovery begins.

**Stage 5: Recovery (Week 4+)**
With consistent execution and managed emotions, the equity curve starts recovering. Confidence rebuilds — slowly, from evidence, not from hope.

Understanding this arc helps you navigate it. When you are in Stage 2 (frustration), you can recognize it: "I am in the frustration stage. This is when I am most likely to make impulsive decisions. I need to slow down, not speed up."

## The Recovery Protocol: 7 Steps

### Step 1: Stop the Bleeding

If you have hit your maximum daily, weekly, or monthly drawdown limit (defined in your trading plan), stop trading. This is not optional. Close your platform. Step away.

If you do not have drawdown limits, establish them now:
- Daily max loss: 2-3% of account
- Weekly max loss: 5-6% of account
- Monthly max loss: 10% of account

These limits exist for one reason: to prevent a bad streak from becoming a catastrophe.

### Step 2: Forensic Review (Not Emotional Review)

Wait 24-48 hours after your last losing trade before reviewing. Emotions need time to settle. Then conduct a forensic analysis:

```
DRAWDOWN FORENSIC REVIEW

Period: _____ to _____
Starting account value: EUR _____
Ending account value: EUR _____
Total drawdown: EUR _____ (___%)
Number of trades during drawdown: _____
Number of winners: _____ (___%)
Number of losers: _____ (___%)

CATEGORIZE EACH LOSING TRADE:

Category A — Good trade, bad outcome:
Setup met all criteria, execution was clean, just lost.
These are expected and do not require changes.
Trades in this category: _____

Category B — Rule violation:
Entered without full criteria, sized wrong, moved stop,
or took the trade for emotional reasons.
Trades in this category: _____

Category C — External factor:
News event, flash crash, liquidity gap — something unusual
and unforeseeable.
Trades in this category: _____

ANALYSIS:
If mostly Category A: System is working. This is a normal
drawdown. Stay the course with reduced size.

If mostly Category B: The drawdown is behavioral, not
strategic. The fix is discipline, not a new strategy.

If mostly Category C: Review your exposure to tail risk.
Consider reducing position sizes during high-uncertainty
periods.
```

### Step 3: Reduce Position Size

During recovery, reduce your position size by 50%. This serves two purposes:

1. **Financial:** Smaller positions mean smaller losses while you rebuild confidence and confirm your system is working.

2. **Psychological:** With half-size positions, each trade carries less emotional weight. You can execute more calmly, which is exactly what you need when your confidence is shaken.

Stay at reduced size until you have had two consecutive winning weeks with proper process execution. Then gradually increase back to full size over the following two weeks.

### Step 4: Return to Fundamentals

During a drawdown recovery, simplify everything. Trade only your highest-conviction setups. On the best instruments. During the best times. No experimental trades. No marginal setups. No new strategies.

Think of it as a musician going back to scales after a bad performance. You are rebuilding from the foundation.

**The A+ Setup Filter:**
During recovery, only take trades that score 5/5 on your setup criteria. Normal times might allow 4/5. Recovery demands perfection in setup quality.

### Step 5: Rebuild Confidence Through Process, Not Profits

During recovery, your primary metric is not P&L — it is process score.

Track your process score daily. Aim for 23+/25 on every trade. When you are executing with high process quality consistently, the profits will follow. Focusing on profits during a drawdown creates desperation, which degrades execution, which extends the drawdown.

**The daily affirmation for recovery:**
"Today I will follow my process perfectly. The outcome is irrelevant. If I execute well, I have won regardless of the P&L."

## What NOT to Do During a Drawdown

### Do Not Change Your Strategy

The most destructive response to a drawdown is to abandon your strategy and adopt a new one. This is called "strategy hopping" and it is a death spiral.

Here is what happens:
1. Strategy A enters a drawdown
2. You switch to Strategy B
3. Strategy A starts recovering (without you)
4. Strategy B enters a drawdown (you hit the bad period)
5. You switch to Strategy C
6. Repeat until account is gone

Every strategy has drawdown periods. If you switch strategies every time you hit a drawdown, you will experience every strategy's worst period and none of their best periods.

**Exception:** If your forensic review reveals a fundamental flaw in your strategy (not just a losing streak), then modification is appropriate. But modification means adjusting a parameter, not throwing everything away and starting over.

### Do Not Increase Size to Recover Faster

This is revenge trading wearing a respectable suit. "If I double my size, I will recover in half the time." True — but if you hit another losing streak at double size, you will blow up in half the time too.

Recovery takes time. Accept this. The math of compounding means that consistent small gains will recover a drawdown faster than you think, as long as you do not make it worse first.

### Do Not Isolate

Drawdowns are lonely. Nobody wants to talk about losing money. But isolation intensifies the emotional impact.

If you have a trading mentor, coach, or accountability partner, this is when you need them. Not for strategy advice — for perspective. Someone who can say: "Yes, drawdowns happen. Yes, yours is within normal range. Yes, following your process is the right response."

### Do Not Stop Journaling

This is the time when your journal is most valuable. The entries you make during a drawdown — capturing your emotional state, your thought process, your temptations — are gold for your future self. When the next drawdown hits, you can reread these entries and remember: "I have been here before. I survived. I recovered. I follow the process."

## The Recovery Mindset

Recovering from a drawdown is fundamentally about trust — trusting your system, trusting your process, and trusting that the math will work over time.

That trust is built by:
- Having a system with a proven edge (backtested, forward-tested, journaled)
- Having survived previous drawdowns (experience compounds)
- Having a written plan that includes drawdown protocols
- Having a journal that documents your growth

If you do not yet have these things, build them now — while your account is healthy and your emotions are stable. Preparing for drawdowns before they happen is infinitely easier than figuring it out in the middle of one.

## Chapter 9 Key Takeaways

- Drawdowns follow a predictable emotional arc: denial, frustration, fear, acceptance, recovery
- The 7-step recovery protocol: stop bleeding, forensic review, reduce size, simplify, focus on process, track recovery, document
- Never change strategies, increase size, or isolate during a drawdown
- Categorize losses: good trades with bad outcomes vs. rule violations vs. external factors
- Reduce to 50% size during recovery; return to full size after 2 consecutive winning weeks
- Trust the process — it is built for drawdowns. Follow it.
