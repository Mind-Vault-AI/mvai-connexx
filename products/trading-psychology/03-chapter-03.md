# Chapter 3: The Decision Journal — Making Better Choices Under Pressure

## Why You Need a Decision Journal (Not Just a Trade Journal)

A trade journal records what happened: entry, exit, P&L. A decision journal records why you made the decisions you made, what you were thinking and feeling, and what information you had at the time.

This distinction matters enormously. A trade journal tells you your results. A decision journal tells you why you got those results and how to improve them.

The concept comes from Annie Duke (professional poker player turned decision scientist) and has been adapted by top hedge fund managers. The core insight: you cannot improve your decision-making by looking only at outcomes, because outcomes in probabilistic environments are partially driven by luck. You need to evaluate the quality of the decision separately from its result.

## The Decision Journal Framework

For every trade, record these elements BEFORE the outcome is known:

### At Entry

```
DECISION JOURNAL ENTRY

Date/Time: _______________
Instrument: _______________
Direction: Long / Short

1. WHAT IS MY THESIS?
   (In one sentence, why am I taking this trade?)
   _________________________________________________

2. WHAT EVIDENCE SUPPORTS THIS?
   (List specific technical/fundamental evidence)
   a. _________________________________________________
   b. _________________________________________________
   c. _________________________________________________

3. WHAT EVIDENCE CONTRADICTS THIS?
   (Devil's advocate — what could go wrong?)
   a. _________________________________________________
   b. _________________________________________________
   c. _________________________________________________

4. WHAT IS MY EDGE?
   (Why does this setup have positive expected value?)
   _________________________________________________

5. WHAT IS MY EMOTIONAL STATE? (circle one)
   Calm / Excited / Anxious / Frustrated / Neutral / FOMO

6. CONFIDENCE LEVEL: ___% (50-100%)
   (50% = coin flip, 100% = certain. Be honest.)

7. WHAT WOULD CHANGE MY MIND?
   (Pre-define your invalidation level)
   _________________________________________________

8. TRADE PARAMETERS:
   Entry: ___________
   Stop Loss: ___________
   Target 1: ___________
   Target 2: ___________
   Position Size: ___________
   Risk Amount: EUR ___________
   R:R Ratio: ___________
```

### At Exit

```
9. EXIT DETAILS:
   Exit Price: ___________
   Exit Reason: Stop hit / Target hit / Manual exit / Time stop
   P&L: EUR ___________
   R Multiple: ___________

10. PROCESS SCORE (rate 1-5):
    Entry quality: ___
    Size correctness: ___
    Stop discipline: ___
    Trade management: ___
    Exit discipline: ___
    TOTAL: ___/25

11. WHAT DID I DO WELL?
    _________________________________________________

12. WHAT WOULD I DO DIFFERENTLY?
    _________________________________________________

13. WAS MY CONFIDENCE LEVEL APPROPRIATE?
    (Was I overconfident or underconfident given the outcome
    and the evidence I had?)
    _________________________________________________
```

## Using the Journal for Pattern Recognition

The real power of the decision journal emerges after 30-50 entries. That is when you can start analyzing your own patterns.

### Monthly Review Analysis

At the end of each month, review all your journal entries and answer:

**Win/Loss Pattern Analysis:**
- What percentage of trades with confidence > 70% were winners?
- What percentage of trades with confidence < 60% were winners?
- Is your confidence calibrated? (If you say 70% confident, do roughly 70% of those trades win?)
- If your confidence is consistently miscalibrated, adjust your sizing accordingly.

**Emotional State Analysis:**
- What was your win rate when you recorded your state as "Calm"?
- What was your win rate when you recorded "Excited" or "FOMO"?
- Which emotional states produce the best and worst results?

**Time Analysis:**
- What time of day produces your best trades?
- What day of the week produces your best trades?
- Are there times you should avoid trading?

**Strategy Analysis:**
- Which setup types (breakout, pullback, reversal, etc.) perform best?
- Which instruments do you trade most profitably?
- What R:R ratio do you achieve in practice (not in theory)?

### The Calibration Exercise

Every quarter, perform this calibration check:

1. Pull all journal entries where your confidence was 60-70%
2. What percentage actually won? It should be approximately 60-70%
3. Pull all entries where confidence was 80%+
4. What percentage won? Should be approximately 80%+

If your 80% confidence trades only win 55% of the time, you are overconfident. This is critical information — it means you should reduce position sizes on trades where you feel very confident, because your feeling of confidence exceeds your actual edge.

If your 60% confidence trades win 75% of the time, you are underconfident. You may be leaving money on the table by sizing too small on trades that are better than you think.

Most traders are overconfident. Knowing this about yourself — quantitatively — is one of the most valuable insights a decision journal can provide.

## The Weekly Review Protocol

Every Sunday (or before the start of your trading week), spend 30-60 minutes on this structured review.

### Step 1: Quantitative Review (10 minutes)

```
WEEKLY REVIEW — Week of _______________

METRICS:
Total trades: ___
Winners: ___ (___%)
Losers: ___ (___%)
Gross profit: EUR ___
Gross loss: EUR ___
Net P&L: EUR ___
Largest winner: EUR ___ (___R)
Largest loser: EUR ___ (___R)
Average winner: EUR ___ (___R)
Average loser: EUR ___ (___R)
Profit factor: ___ (gross profit / gross loss)
Average process score: ___/25
```

### Step 2: Qualitative Review (15 minutes)

```
BEST TRADE OF THE WEEK:
What made it good (process, not just outcome)?
_________________________________________________

WORST TRADE OF THE WEEK:
What went wrong (process, not just outcome)?
_________________________________________________

EMOTIONAL PATTERNS THIS WEEK:
Did any of the 5 traps show up? Which ones?
_________________________________________________

RULES FOLLOWED / BROKEN:
Which rules did I follow consistently?
_________________________________________________
Which rules did I violate? Why?
_________________________________________________
```

### Step 3: Improvement Focus (5 minutes)

```
ONE THING TO IMPROVE NEXT WEEK:
(Choose only ONE — trying to fix everything at once
fixes nothing)
_________________________________________________

HOW I WILL MEASURE THIS:
_________________________________________________

ACCOUNTABILITY:
I will review this on _______________ (date)
```

### Step 4: Market Preparation (15 minutes)

```
NEXT WEEK'S FOCUS:
Key levels to watch: ___________________________
Scheduled news events: _________________________
Setups I am watching: __________________________
Any reason to reduce risk this week? _____________
```

## Chapter 3 Key Takeaways

- A decision journal captures WHY you made decisions, not just what happened
- Record your thesis, evidence, confidence, and emotional state BEFORE you know the outcome
- After 30-50 entries, analyze your patterns: confidence calibration, emotional impact, time patterns
- The weekly review is the single highest-value activity for improving as a trader
- Focus on improving one specific thing per week — not everything at once
- Your journal is your most valuable trading tool — more valuable than any indicator
