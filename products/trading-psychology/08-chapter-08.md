# Chapter 8: The Compound Effect of Small Wins — Why 1% Matters

## The Power of Compounding in Trading

Albert Einstein allegedly called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. Whether he said it or not, the math is undeniable — and it applies to trading returns with even more force than it does to savings accounts.

Most traders chase big wins. They want the 50% return month, the 10R trade, the screenshot-worthy profit. Meanwhile, the consistently profitable traders are quietly compounding 2-4% per month and building wealth that dwarfs the occasional jackpot winner.

## The Compounding Math

### Monthly Compounding Table

Starting capital: EUR 10,000. What happens at different consistent monthly returns over time?

| Monthly Return | After 1 Year | After 2 Years | After 5 Years |
|---------------|-------------|--------------|--------------|
| 1% | EUR 11,268 | EUR 12,697 | EUR 18,167 |
| 2% | EUR 12,682 | EUR 16,084 | EUR 32,810 |
| 3% | EUR 14,258 | EUR 20,328 | EUR 58,916 |
| 4% | EUR 16,010 | EUR 25,633 | EUR 108,548 |
| 5% | EUR 17,959 | EUR 32,251 | EUR 199,145 |

Look at the 3% column. A consistent 3% monthly return turns EUR 10,000 into EUR 58,916 in five years. That is a 489% total return.

Now look at the 5% column. EUR 10,000 becomes EUR 199,145. The difference between 3% and 5% per month — just 2 percentage points — is a 3.4x difference in outcome over five years.

**This is why consistency matters more than big wins.** A trader who makes 3% every single month will dramatically outperform a trader who makes 20% one month, loses 15% the next, makes 5%, loses 10%, and so on — even if the volatile trader's average return is similar.

### The Volatility Tax

Volatility in returns literally costs you money through a mathematical phenomenon called "volatility drag."

**Example:**
- Trader A: Makes 10% one month, loses 10% the next. Average return: 0%.
  - Start: EUR 10,000 → EUR 11,000 → EUR 9,900. Actual return: -1%.
- Trader B: Makes 0.5% every month. Average return: 0.5%.
  - Start: EUR 10,000 → EUR 10,050 → EUR 10,100.25. Actual return: +1%.

Trader A's returns averaged 0% but actually lost 1%. This is not a rounding error — it is a mathematical reality. The more volatile your returns, the more compound growth you sacrifice.

**The lesson:** Smooth, consistent returns always beat volatile returns, even when the averages are the same. This is why drawdown management is not just about survival — it is about maximizing compound growth.

## Building a Small-Win System

### The Daily Target Approach

Instead of thinking about monthly or annual returns, think about daily or per-trade targets.

If your target is 3% per month and you trade 15 days per month:
```
Daily target = 3% / 15 = 0.2% per day
On EUR 10,000: EUR 20 per day
```

EUR 20 per day sounds unimpressive. But it compounds to EUR 4,258 in a year. And the psychological benefit is enormous: EUR 20 is achievable on almost any day, which reduces pressure and improves execution.

### The R-Target Approach

An even better approach: target a specific number of R per week.

```
If you risk 1% per trade (EUR 100 on a EUR 10,000 account):
Target: +3R per week

That means you need net 3 units of risk in profit per week.
Examples:
- 3 winning trades at 1R each
- 1 winning trade at 5R and 2 losing trades at -1R
- 4 winning trades at 1.5R and 3 losing trades at -1R

At +3R per week x 50 weeks = +150R per year
150R x EUR 100 = EUR 15,000 profit per year
(150% return on a EUR 10,000 account)
```

The R-target approach is superior because it does not require you to profit every day. It allows for losing days and weeks within the context of an overall positive expectancy.

## The Mathematics of Streaks

### Losing Streaks Are Inevitable

Even with a 60% win rate, losing streaks of 5+ trades will happen regularly. Here is the probability:

| Win Rate | P(3 losses in a row) | P(5 in a row) | P(7 in a row) | P(10 in a row) |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|---------------|----------------|
| 40% | 21.6% | 7.8% | 2.8% | 0.6% |
| 50% | 12.5% | 3.1% | 0.8% | 0.1% |
| 55% | 9.1% | 1.8% | 0.4% | 0.03% |
| 60% | 6.4% | 1.0% | 0.2% | 0.01% |

Even at a 60% win rate, there is a 1% chance of 5 consecutive losses in any given sequence of trades. Over the course of 200 trades per year, you will almost certainly experience at least one such streak.

**This is normal.** This is not your strategy breaking. This is statistics.

### Winning Streaks Are Equally Inevitable

The flip side: at a 55% win rate, you will also experience winning streaks of 5+ trades. The key is to treat them identically — with consistent position sizing and process adherence.

The trader who survives losing streaks and capitalizes on winning streaks (with consistent sizing, not increased sizing) will compound their account steadily over time.

## The Power of Cutting Losers and Running Winners

### The Asymmetry That Creates Wealth

The single most important behavioral pattern in profitable trading:

**Cut losers short. Let winners run.**

This is easy to say and psychologically brutal to execute. Here is why it matters:

**Scenario A: Average trader behavior**
- Wins: Takes profit quickly at +0.5R to +1R (fear of giving back profit)
- Losses: Holds hoping for recovery, average loss -1.5R to -2R (fear of being wrong)
- Result: Average win < Average loss. Needs >65% win rate to break even. Very difficult.

**Scenario B: Disciplined trader behavior**
- Wins: Holds to targets, lets runners ride. Average win +2R to +3R
- Losses: Exits at stop without hesitation. Average loss -1R
- Result: Average win >> Average loss. Needs only 33-40% win rate. Very achievable.

The math:
```
Scenario A: 60% win rate x 0.8R average win - 40% x 1.5R average loss
= 0.48 - 0.60 = -0.12R per trade (LOSING despite 60% win rate!)

Scenario B: 45% win rate x 2.5R average win - 55% x 1.0R average loss
= 1.125 - 0.55 = +0.575R per trade (PROFITABLE at only 45% wins!)
```

Scenario B makes money with a LOWER win rate because the average winner is much larger than the average loser. This is the edge that compound growth is built on.

## Practical Strategies for Letting Winners Run

### Strategy 1: Remove the P&L Display

Many profitable traders hide their unrealized P&L while in a trade. They manage based on price levels, not dollar amounts. Seeing "+EUR 500" triggers the urge to close and "lock in" the profit. Seeing "price is at my Target 1 level" triggers the planned partial exit.

### Strategy 2: Set-and-Forget After Entry

Enter the trade with your stop and targets as orders. Close the chart. Walk away. Come back at a scheduled time to manage. This eliminates the minute-by-minute emotional rollercoaster that causes premature exits.

### Strategy 3: The Commitment Device

Tell yourself (or write in your journal): "I will not touch this trade until [specific condition]. If I close early without a valid reason, I will record it as a rule violation and reduce my size tomorrow."

Making the cost of premature exit explicit (rule violation, size reduction) counteracts the short-term emotional reward of taking profit early.

## Compounding Beyond Returns

Compounding applies to more than money:

**Compounding knowledge:** Every trade you journal, every weekly review you complete, every pattern you identify adds to your understanding. After 200 journaled trades, your pattern recognition is exponentially better than after 20.

**Compounding discipline:** Every time you follow your rules despite emotional pressure, it gets slightly easier the next time. Discipline is a muscle that strengthens with use.

**Compounding confidence:** Real confidence (not overconfidence) comes from a track record of consistent execution. After 6 months of following your plan, your conviction in your system is unshakeable — not because you always win, but because you know the math works.
